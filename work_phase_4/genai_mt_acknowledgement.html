Case Study 2: Component Design

This case presents the development process of a three-month responsive timetable module, with support from GenAI tools. The project demonstrates how AI-assisted iterations helped refine UI layout, responsiveness, and user interaction logic‚Äîfrom a mobile-first calendar design to enhanced background customization features.

Requirement Specification

My Submission (Initial Request):

"Design a timetable interface with three calendar tables for May, June, and July 2025, rendered inside <section class='timetable'>. Display June by default, with arrows for switching months. Each day cell should support content insertion below the date label."

Evaluation

My Technical Assessment:

Functional Correctness:

Confirmed that month switching logic worked correctly with arrow controls and that content could be injected per cell.

Verified calendar days render in correct alignment using JS and dynamic date generation.

Functional Issues:

Detected layout overflow on mobile view due to large table width.

Observed that the calendar header (month + arrows) scrolled away when swiping the table horizontally on small screens.

Noted that even empty day cells appeared with unintended background shading.

Problem-Driven AI Iteration

My Follow-Up Request:

Based on the evaluation, I refined my prompt to request a mobile-first CSS structure using media queries for desktop support. I also asked for a fixed calendar header that remains centered while the table scrolls on mobile.

Lastly, I requested the ability to assign custom background colors to specific date cells while keeping text background transparent‚Äîthis enabled clearer visual markers for event days.

GenAI Reflection

This section presents my reflections on using GenAI throughout the development process. It outlines key takeaways and practical strategies for effectively integrating AI support within a structured workflow.

üí°Modular Thinking and Task Delegation

One major insight I gained is the value of establishing a clear design structure before involving AI. Instead of assigning large, complex tasks all at once, I found it much more effective to divide the work into well-defined, modular components.

For example, in Case Study 1, rather than asking the AI to create an entirely new form with an image upload feature, I asked it to add an upload function to a form I had already built. This significantly reduced debugging complexity and made subsequent adjustments faster and more efficient.

By guiding AI to complete specific and narrowly scoped modules, I achieved more consistent and higher-quality outcomes. This modular approach also made it easier to spot and fix issues, avoiding the inefficiencies that often arise when AI-generated work drifts away from the original design intent.

üí°Comprehension Through Visualization

Another essential strategy is making AI-generated outputs more transparent and easier to understand. To maintain control over the process, I often asked the AI to include explanatory comments, console logs, or visual indicators to clarify what was being implemented.

These aids helped me grasp what instructions the AI followed, what logic it applied, and how the system behavior evolved. Sometimes, AI fails to recognize its own mistakes‚Äîonly through clear visualization and thorough understanding can users effectively evaluate and guide the output.

Full comprehension of AI-generated contributions is critical for maintaining design coherence, making informed decisions, and avoiding wasted effort due to misinterpretation or hidden errors.
